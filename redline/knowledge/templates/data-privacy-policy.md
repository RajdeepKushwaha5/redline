## 1. Purpose

This policy establishes the principles, requirements, and procedures for the collection, use, storage, and disposal of personal data across the organization. It ensures compliance with applicable data protection regulations including the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to all employees, contractors, and third parties who access, process, or manage personal data on behalf of the organization. It covers personal data in all formats — digital and physical.

## 3. Definitions

- **Personal Data**: Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person.
- **Special Category Data**: Data revealing racial/ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data, health data, or data concerning sex life or sexual orientation.
- **Data Subject**: The individual whose personal data is processed.
- **Data Controller**: The organization that determines the purposes and means of processing.
- **Data Processor**: A third party that processes data on behalf of the controller.
- **Processing**: Any operation performed on personal data (collection, recording, storage, retrieval, use, disclosure, erasure).

## 5. Data Subject Rights

The organization respects and facilitates the following data subject rights:

### 5.1 Right of Access (Article 15)
Data subjects may request a copy of their personal data. Requests must be fulfilled within 30 calendar days. No fee unless requests are manifestly unfounded or excessive.

### 5.2 Right to Rectification (Article 16)
Data subjects may request correction of inaccurate data. Corrections must be made within 30 calendar days.

### 5.3 Right to Erasure (Article 17)
Data subjects may request deletion when: data is no longer necessary, consent is withdrawn, data was unlawfully processed, or erasure is required by law. Exceptions apply where processing is required for legal obligations or public interest.

### 5.4 Right to Restrict Processing (Article 18)
Data subjects may request restriction while accuracy is contested, processing is unlawful, or the organization no longer needs the data but the subject needs it for legal claims.

### 5.5 Right to Data Portability (Article 20)
Data subjects may receive their data in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format (e.g., CSV, JSON). Where technically feasible, data will be transmitted directly to another controller upon request.

### 5.6 Right to Object (Article 21)
Data subjects may object to processing based on legitimate interests or public interest. Processing must cease unless compelling legitimate grounds override the subject's interests.

### 5.7 Rights Related to Automated Decision-Making (Article 22)
Data subjects have the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing — including profiling — that produce legal effects or similarly significant effects. Where automated decisions are made, the organization provides:
- Meaningful information about the logic involved
- The significance and envisaged consequences
- The right to obtain human intervention
- The right to express their point of view
- The right to contest the decision

### 5.8 Process for Exercising Rights
Requests should be submitted to [privacy contact/DPO email]. All requests are logged, verified for identity, and responded to within 30 calendar days. Extensions of up to 60 additional days are permitted for complex requests, with notification to the requester within the initial 30 days.

## 6. Consent

Where consent is the lawful basis:
- Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous
- Consent records are maintained including: who consented, when, what they were told, how they consented
- Withdrawal must be as easy as giving consent — via the same channel or simpler
- For children under 16 (or 13 where member state law permits), parental consent is required

## 7. Data Protection Impact Assessment

A DPIA is required before any processing that is likely to result in high risk, including:
- Systematic and extensive evaluation of personal aspects (profiling)
- Large-scale processing of special category data
- Systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas
- Use of new technologies with potential high risk
- Automated decision-making with legal or significant effects

The DPO must be consulted during the DPIA. If the DPIA identifies residual high risk that cannot be mitigated, the supervisory authority must be consulted (Article 36).

## 8. Data Breach Notification

### 8.1 Internal Reporting
All suspected breaches must be reported to the security team within 4 hours of discovery via [incident reporting channel].

### 8.2 Supervisory Authority Notification (Article 33)
The DPO must notify the supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware of a breach that poses a risk to data subject rights and freedoms. The notification must include: nature of the breach, data categories affected, approximate number of subjects, likely consequences, and mitigation measures.

### 8.3 Data Subject Notification (Article 34)
Data subjects must be notified without undue delay when the breach is likely to result in high risk to their rights and freedoms. Notification is not required if: data was encrypted, subsequent measures eliminated the risk, or individual notification would involve disproportionate effort (public communication instead).

## 9. Cross-Border Data Transfers

Personal data must not be transferred outside the European Economic Area (EEA) unless:
- The destination country has an adequacy decision from the European Commission (Article 45)
- Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) are in place (Article 46(2)(c)) with a transfer impact assessment
- Binding Corporate Rules are approved (Article 47)
- A derogation applies (Article 49) — explicit consent, contract necessity, or important public interest

Transfer impact assessments must evaluate the legal framework of the receiving country and identify supplementary measures where necessary.

## 11. Privacy by Design and Default

- Data protection must be integrated into system design from the outset, not added afterward
- Default settings must be the most privacy-protective (collect minimum data, restrict access by default)
- Privacy impact must be assessed during system procurement, development, and change management
- Data minimization: only collect data that is necessary for the stated purpose

## 13. Records of Processing Activities

The organization maintains a Record of Processing Activities (Article 30) including:
- Processing purposes
- Categories of data subjects and personal data
- Recipients of personal data
- Transfers to third countries
- Retention periods
- Description of technical and organizational security measures

The register is reviewed quarterly and made available to the supervisory authority upon request.

## 14. Third-Party Processor Agreements

All third-party processors must have a written Data Processing Agreement (Article 28) containing:
- Processing only on documented instructions from the controller
- Confidentiality obligations for processing personnel
- Appropriate technical and organizational security measures
- Assistance with data subject rights requests
- Assistance with DPIA and breach notification obligations
- Deletion or return of data on termination
- Audit rights for the controller
- Sub-processor approval and flow-down of obligations
